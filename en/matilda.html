Jocelyn Bell Burnell

The Discovery of Pulsars

Susan Jocelyn Bell Burnell, born on July 15, 1943 in Belfast, is a British astrophysicist whose discovery revolutionized our understanding of the universe. In 1967, while completing her PhD at Cambridge University, she detected the first pulsar, a discovery that would win the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1974… but not for her.

What is a pulsar?

A Revolutionary Discovery

In November 1967, Jocelyn Bell noticed an unusual signal in the data from the radio telescope she had helped build. This pulsed signal, extremely regular, came from a fixed point in the sky. Initially nicknamed "LGM-1", it turned out to be the very first pulsar ever detected.

Pulsars are rapidly rotating neutron stars that emit beams of electromagnetic radiation. Her discovery opened a new field of study in astrophysics.

The Matilda Effect in Action

In 1974, the Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Antony Hewish and Martin Ryle. Jocelyn Bell, despite being at the origin of the discovery of pulsars, was excluded from this recognition.

This case became an example of the Matilda Effect: the minimization of women scientists' contributions. Several scientists criticized this decision.

"The Nobel Prize is given for scientific leadership and not for the discovery itself."

An Inspiring Legacy

Despite this injustice, Jocelyn Bell Burnell pursued a brilliant career. In 2018, she received the Special Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics (3 million dollars) and donated the entire amount to create scholarships.

Her story continues to inspire and reminds us of the importance of fairly recognizing the contributions of all researchers.
